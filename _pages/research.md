I've been working in a variety of research settings for almost seven years now. While many of my previous roles don't have a direct link to my current area of research, behavioral neuroendocrinology, each of these experiences has shaped who I am as a scientist and are absolutely worth mentioning. 


**PhD Research**

Broadly, I study how oxytocin, vasopressin, and HPA activity interact to impact social behavior. I am particularly interested in how stress at different phases of development can lead to dysregulations of the oxytocin and vasopressin systems, and how these dysregulations can contribute to stress reactivity and other components of psychiatric disease later in life. I am also interested in whether these effects persist across generations and whether there are epigenetic mechanisms that underly this. 

To answer these questions, I use the eusocial naked mole-rat (NMR). NMRs are small, subterranean rodents native to East Africa that engage in an extreme form of mammalian cooperative breeding. They live in colonies up to 300 individuals, where breeding is limited to one breeding female (“queen”) and one to three breeding males (“breeders”). All other animals in the colony (“subordinates”) are kept reproductively suppressed by the presence of dominance and other behaviors exhibited by the queen. Most (~99%) subordinates never undergo puberty in the wild, but in the lab, we can induce puberty by removing a subordinate from its colony and pairing it with an opposite-sex mate. The ability to experimentally manipulate puberty, coupled with the ability to capitalize on the extreme yet stable social phenotypes exhibited by NMRs, makes NMRs an excellent model for studying the neurobiology of social behavior. 


**Previous Research**

At Buffalo State College, I got my feet wet in a variety of research settings within the department of psychology. I first worked in Dr. Stephani Foraker's **Cognition and Communication Lab** as a volunteer and summer fellowship student, where I studied the impact of individual differences in working memory on language comprehension. I then worked in Dr. Jean DiPirro's **Behavioral Neuroscience Lab** as a Ronald E. McNair Scholar, where I completed an honors thesis studying the impact of acute and chronic MDMA ("Ecstasy") administration on social behavior in adolescent rats. 

After earning my B.A, I worked as a research technician at the University at Buffalo (UB), namely Dr. Matthew Paul's **Behavioral Neuroendocrinology Lab**. Here, I helped with a variety of studies on how neuropeptides and sex hormones regulate adolescent social behavior in Siberian hamsters and the vasopressin-deficient Brattleboro Rat, including one project where I characterized estrogen receptor alpha in the ventromedial hypothalamus of male and female Siberian hamsters reared in different seasonal environments. 

After about a year as a research technician, I decided to continue my education as a master's student at UB. I joined Dr. Peter Thanos' **Behavioral Neuropharmacology and Neuroimaging Lab**, located at the **Clinical and Research Institute on Addictions** on UB's medical campus. My thesis focused on the impact of chronic oral methylphenidate (Ritalin) treatment on behavior and physiology in adolescent rats. I also spent a substantial amount of time on a side project investigating the behavioral and neurochemical effects of vaporized THC exposure during adolescence, and another on the impact of bariatric surgery on subsequent alcohol abuse. Throughout my M.A., I learned a variety of laboratory techniques including surgery (jugular vein catherization and stereotaxic brain surgery), small animal micro positron emission tomography (microPET), and many different behavioral testing paradigms, including operant IV self-administration. 


**What happens next?**

While the current academic job climate looks bleak and I am currently assessing whether conducting research will remain a component of my career after I finish my PhD, I do have some interests that I would like to pursue in the future. I am particularly interested in the neural changes associated with social deficits in a variety of neurodevelopmental and psychiatric disorders, including ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, anxiety, and addiction. I would love to bridge together my MA and PhD work by using traditional behavioral neuroendocrinology techniques coupled with small animal neuroimaging. I am also a huge fan of translational research and am strongly considering the possibility of dabbling in human research again!
